Songs to "normal people"

Brassens wrote many songs dedicated to "normal" or "small people". He would either celebrate their good deeds or just reflect on their lives with sensitivity and empathy. His rules would also apply to this category of songs: take an original angle, polish the wording to the extreme, make sure the music underlines the lyrics. I would add one feature: delicacy. Empathy never gets heavy, it remains subtle and, more often than not, peppered with a hint of humour or gentle irony.

The most famous song of this category is certainly La Chanson pour l’Auvergnat, a tribute to his very accommodating landlord, which celebrates simple people who show empathy to outsiders. But one of my favs is much less famous, which is a shame if you ask me: Pénélope, in which Brassens muses on the unspoken dreams of a woman who lives a happy if perhaps uneventful life with her husband. Some will assume that Brassens tries to tempt her or doubts her faithfulness. I disagree. To me, he just examines her life with genuine interest, wondering about her feelings, hoping perhaps that she nurtures more passionate dreams as healthy tokens of a rich inner life, but also showing respect to her lifestyle. Of course, the character of the song is named after the ancient Greek Penelope, Ulysses’ wife, who remained faithful for 20 years while her husband was fighting the Trojan war and then facing Poseidon[1]'s hostility on his way back home. Why do I like this song so much? Because I love the melody, although it took me quite a while to sing it right - it’s actually quite subtle. Because Brassens' linguistic inventiveness is at its best from the first to the last line (which means that you will have to put up with many comments!). And, most of all, because both the lyrics and the music convey Brassens' empathy with the most exquisite delicacy.

Le Petit Joueur de Flûteau (The Little Piper)

I remember not being carried away by this song as a child. Much later, I completely changed my mind. The theme perfectly illustrates Brassens' taste for simplicity and loyalty, rather than fame and money. Not just Brassens', by the way. This wariness, even suspicion towards money and fame as corrupting agents is typically French. Not entirely unfounded, although sometimes a bit excessive. The title, as well as the music and lyrics of the first two lines, lure the audience into expecting a somewhat naïve song, a tale for children. But then the full Brassens arsenal is deployed: an original angle, hugely creative lyrics highlighted by a not-so-simple melody. Long story short, this has become one of my (many) favourite Brassens songs.

It tells about a musician from a little village, who is offered fame and ennoblement by the king because his songs are so beautiful. He refuses because he fears it might cause him to despise his origins and lose his soul. Of course, this musician is Brassens himself as he wanted to remain. And indeed, although he bought a nice house and a car at one point in his life, he did stick to the Little Piper’s philosophy. This is one of the reasons, in addition to his dazzling talent, why he is such a legend in France.

The language of the song is borrowed from the traditional way of speaking in modest country backgrounds. No longer spoken today, it sounds both naïve and elegant to our modern ears – and most endearing.

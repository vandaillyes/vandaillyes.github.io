Le Gorille

Introduction

As mentioned above, Brassens wrote Le Gorille early in his career – in 1952. It’s a stunning showcase of his talent as a wordsmith, but also of his intelligence: the scenario/language combination is hilarious, and the song is much more meaningful than it seems – its true meaning is very cleverly unveiled by the very last line.[1] I want to insist on this: there is some humour in the absurdity of the story, but the real fun resides in the elaborate language he uses to tell it, completely at odds with the contents and at the same time so apt to unravel events before our eyes with originality and accuracy, keeping the song well away from any vulgarity in spite of the rather trashy scenario.

I’ve known this song by heart for decades. Still, whenever I hear it or sing it in my head, I can’t help laughing out loud. It’s simply the funniest song I know. I will translate and comment the most linguistically inventive parts, trying to convey their humorous impact – although I’m aware it’s probably impossible to fully enjoy it unless French is your native language. I intentionally kept the translation close to the French structure, closer than a "natural-sounding" English version would require. The purpose is to help the "French-as-a-foreign-language" speaker understand some specificities of Brassens' writing.

Some additional notes about the song.

Phrasing

I strongly recommend listening to the song, of course. As always, Brassens uses the traditional phrasing of poetry, whereby mute "e"s are pronounced when followed by consonants (the rare exceptions are signalled by replacing the "e" by an apostrophe). Also from his poet’s toolkit, he does not hesitate to cut a line mid-phrase or even mid-word. This gives him more flexibility to find good rhymes, but also increases the impact of the next line by suspending it – the melody is always designed to help that. Le Gorille features a couple of these: "obligé de / Violer un juge…", "Je ne peux / Pas la dire…". But in my view, the most striking examples are found in La Non-Demande en Mariage and Les Passantes,[53] both discussed further in this series.

Now here is a feature that is more specific to Le Gorille: with many fast-repeated notes and a uniform speech, it sounds surprisingly close to rap. Pretty trash rap in a way – not many rappers go as far as talking about zoophilia! With its eight-note bursts, the flow is different to today’s rap, but the lyrics' phrasing provides remarkable cadence. It often consists in hard consonants coupled with very different vowels, which gives punch and clarity to each syllable in spite of the speed of delivery. The best examples of this effect are:

  • "C’est complètement impossible" (pronouncing the bolded "e" and "t")

  • "C’est j’en suis convaincu la vieille"

  • "Supposez qu’un de vous puisse être"

Note that the first two lines above purposefully refer to each other: they both say that the ape should not go for the judge, and they are very similar in form: hard consonants, not twice the same vowel, and the use of the three nasal sounds "on, en, in".

On the other hand, Brassens can also play with the repetition of vowels ("Malheureusement je ne peux") – another way to feed the flow.

Message

I won’t dwell on the fact that challenging death penalty in the 50s was quite visionary. What I want to marvel at is how he maximised the impact of the message via its form. Several elements here.

The most obvious one is how he lures the listener into a seemingly absurd story, makes him laugh to tears before delivering the abrupt punch line. Surprise creates impact.

Another element is a bit lazier but proved quite efficient: by being provocative with a tale that was bound to be deemed obscene, he was sure to create the buzz. And, of course, it worked.

Finally, obviously, language: a key element of the song’s devastating humour, it greatly contributed to its popularity, which spread the message. Its quality also disarmed those who were shocked by the contents, keeping the song immune to any accusation of vulgarity.

Perhaps I am over-analysing here. Maybe I should just ask: can you think of a more original, clever, striking way to address such a grim topic as death penalty?

Conclusion

Le Gorille was immensely successful in spite (or because) of the scandal it caused. It was banned from the national radio for a while, but it didn’t hinder its overwhelming success. It belongs to the album La Mauvaise Réputation, which catapulted Brassens to stardom almost overnight after a long period of struggle. In my view, it remains one of Brassens' best satirical songs (songs that challenged the morality of the time), showcasing Brassens’s dazzling skills at:

  • Choosing truly original angles to address topics

  • Combining perfect classic form and creativity to create wonderful lyrics: elegance, imagery, originality, amazing rhymes and alliterations, and perfect flow – in short, true poetry.

  • Using the above to describe absurd situations or deliver politically incorrect messages

  • Matching lyrics and music to create a compelling flow

Also recommended in this category:

  • Mourir pour des Idées, about leaders who take their people to death for the sake of their ideals. A bit controversial after a period that proved that fighting a war was sometimes unavoidable, but still so relevant, and so brilliantly crafted!

  • À l’Ombre des Maris, about adultery: starts as an apology defending adultererous women, then swerves towards the crazy story of a man who becomes friends with his mistresses' husbands.

  • Quatre-vingt quinze fois sur cent (la femme s’emmerde en baisant) about men’s vanity when it comes to sex. I particularly recommend the first verse, a wordcraft masterpiece that turns out to be a hilarious introduction to the topic, helped by a brilliant match with the music.

  • La Mauvaise Réputation, about people’s defiance towards those who think/behave differently, with the famous lines:

Non les brav' gens[54] n’aiment pas que
L’on suive une autre route qu’eux

No, good people do not like it when
You follow a different path to theirs

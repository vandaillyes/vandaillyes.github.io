Love songs

The next songs I want to share are in a totally different mood: exit burlesque, enter romance and poetry, with La Non Demande en Mariage and Les Passantes. Let me start with a general word about Brassens' romantic love songs. They show how wrong the general perception is about Brassens' melodies, often assumed to be "basic", "secondary to lyrics" and "all alike". Just try singing any of his songs with some accuracy! (For the record, his guitar parts frighten even pro guitarists because he never follows standard chord patterns…). Brassens does have a very specific musical style, with strong harmonic and rhythmical features that make his songs sound alike to newcomers. Just as you may think that all Chinese people look alike! But if you dig in, you find how different (and often complex) they are.

The melodies of his romance songs illustrate this point because they also have more conspicuous differences when compared with his other songs: they are more "obviously" romantic, beautifully underlined by a second guitar and a double bass. But they keep Brassens' trademark: their capacity to maximise the impact of lyrics. That flow, that mood, and those "hanging in the air" moments where a note keeps you waiting for the next word… irresistible.

La Non Demande en Mariage (The Non Proposal of Marriage)

So… La Non Demande en Mariage is a super romantic love letter to Brassens' life-long lover and muse Joha Heiman, aka Püppchen. As the song suggests, they never married nor lived under the same roof – although their relationship remained very strong and faithful until his death. Brassens wrote several songs for Püppchen. La Non Demande en Mariage is my favourite, on par with the wonderful Saturne – the first two verses of which I will add as a bonus…

Les Passantes

Our second love song is Les Passantes, about women you only meet for a few moments but who leave a lasting impression on you – the "feeling of a possibility". Brassens didn’t write the lyrics but fell in love with this poem by Antoine Pol as early as 1940. He made a song of it but, characteristically, reworked it for decades. In the 60s, he vainly tried to contact Pol to get his approval before (finally) publishing the song. Strange coincidence: in 1971, it’s Pol who contacted Brassens because he wanted to publish a book about him! He then learnt about the song Brassens had made with Les Passantes but, sadly, died before he had a chance to listen to it. What a shame, because it’s a masterpiece…

Pol’s language is more straightforward than Brassens', therefore my comments will be shorter than usual. But, wonderfully highlighted by the music, this poem is irresistibly romantic… The romantic melody and Brassens' perfect diction, slightly "after the beat", give each word a special treatment. And, better than in any other song, the music treats us with "hanging moments", delays before key words that give them so much impact!

Saturne

Now, I promised to translate and comment parts of Saturne. Culture-riddled, featuring beautiful lyrics carried by a romantic and elaborate melody, it’s a trademark Brassens love song, dedicated to his ageing lover Püppchen. Saturn (Kronos for the ancient Greek, often confused with Chronos) was a Titan, master of Time[43] and father of Jupiter/Zeus. Having been told that he would be overthrown by one of his sons, he had the questionable habit of devouring his male offspring (to no avail: Jupiter was saved by his mother and did overthrow his father). This, added to his identification with passing time, explains his association with melancholy and sadness. But he can’t spoil Brassens' love for his Püppchen…

Here is the full song, but I only commented the trickiest phrases… and my favourite verses.
